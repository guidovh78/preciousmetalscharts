How dollar-cost averaging works

Instead of trying to pick the bottom, dollar-cost averaging buys the same money amount on a fixed schedule. When the price is low your fixed amount buys more ounces; when it is high it buys fewer. Over time your average cost lands somewhere in the middle and a single bad entry point matters far less. It is the most common way ordinary savers build a metal position, and this tool shows how it would have played out — including the long flat stretches, not just the good years.

Common questions

What is dollar-cost averaging?

Buying a fixed money amount of metal at regular intervals regardless of price. You buy more ounces when it is cheap and fewer when it is dear, smoothing your average cost and removing the need to time the market.

How is the backtest calculated?

For each month we take the real spot price and buy the entered amount's worth of ounces, then total them. Value today is your ounces times the latest spot price. It uses monthly closes and ignores premiums, fees and taxes.

Does it include premiums and fees?

No — it buys at spot. Real coins and bars carry a premium, and storage or transaction costs reduce returns, so treat the result as an idealised upper bound.

Does the past predict the future?

No. Prices can fall as well as rise and past results do not predict future returns. This is educational information, not investment advice.
