The idea, and the catch

The gold-to-silver ratio swings widely — from the high teens to over 100. The switching idea exploits that: when silver is historically cheap versus gold (a high ratio) you convert gold to silver; when silver is historically dear (a low ratio) you convert back. If the ratio keeps mean-reverting, you accumulate ounces without predicting prices. The catch is friction. Every switch sells one metal at a dealer's buy-back price and buys the other at a premium, often triggers tax, and for silver in the EU usually adds VAT. Turn the cost per switch up to a realistic level and you will see how quickly those costs eat a frictionless edge — which is exactly why this tool includes it.

Common questions

What is the switching strategy?

Trading between gold and silver at ratio extremes to accumulate more total metal: swap to silver when the ratio is high, back to gold when it is low. If the ratio oscillates you can end with more ounces than holding.

Does it beat holding?

Sometimes without costs, but real switches are expensive. Set a realistic cost per switch and the apparent edge often shrinks or disappears. It is an illustration, not a recommendation.

What does a real switch cost?

A buy-back below spot on what you sell, a premium above spot on what you buy, shipping, possible tax on gains, and often VAT on silver in the EU. That is why the cost setting matters.

Is this advice?

No. It is a historical illustration of a known idea with its assumptions stated. Past results do not predict the future — educational information only.
