Common questions about precious-metals prices

Short, self-contained answers to the basics most people search for.

What is the spot price of gold?

The spot price of gold is the current market price to buy or sell one troy ounce of unfabricated gold for immediate settlement, set continuously by global trading on venues such as COMEX and the London (LBMA) market. Spot is a reference point; physical coins and bars cost more, because dealers add a premium for fabrication and handling.

What is the gold-to-silver ratio?

The gold-to-silver ratio is the number of ounces of silver it takes to buy one ounce of gold, calculated by dividing the gold price by the silver price. A high ratio is widely read as a sign that silver is cheap relative to gold; the ratio has historically ranged from under 35 at silver peaks to above 100 during market stress.

Why is the price I pay higher than the spot price?

The price you pay for physical metal is higher than spot because of the premium, the markup a dealer charges over the spot reference to cover minting, fabrication, distribution and margin. Premiums are typically larger on small coins than on large bars, and larger on silver than on gold.

Do precious-metal prices change on weekends?

Precious-metal spot prices update during global market hours, roughly Sunday evening through Friday afternoon (US Eastern time), and hold steady when those markets are closed. Dealers may still quote and take orders over the weekend, but the underlying spot reference does not move until trading reopens.

What is a troy ounce in grams?

A troy ounce is the standard unit for pricing precious metals and equals 31.1035 grams — heavier than the everyday (avoirdupois) ounce of 28.35 grams. Gold, silver, platinum and palladium are all quoted per troy ounce, so the price per gram is simply the troy-ounce price divided by 31.1035.

What makes gold, silver and platinum prices move?

Precious-metal prices move with the US dollar, real interest rates, inflation expectations and central-bank buying. Gold is driven mainly by monetary factors and safe-haven demand, while silver, platinum and palladium also respond to industrial demand — solar, electronics and autocatalysts — which tends to make them more volatile than gold.
