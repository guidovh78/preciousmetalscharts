01

How the premium works

When you buy a physical coin or bar, you pay more than the raw metal is worth. That gap is the premium — it pays for minting, fabrication, distribution and the dealer's margin. Two products with the same metal weight can carry very different premiums: small coins cost more per ounce than large bars, and well-known government coins usually carry more than plain rounds. The premium is simply your price minus the metal's spot value, shown as money and as a percentage of that spot value.

03

Common questions

What is a premium over spot?

The premium is the amount you pay above the spot price when you buy a physical coin or bar. It covers minting, fabrication, distribution and the dealer's margin. Premium = your price minus the spot value of the metal it contains.

How do I calculate the premium on a gold coin?

Multiply the spot price per troy ounce by the coin's metal weight to get its spot value, then subtract that from the price you are paying. Divide the difference by the spot value for the percentage. This tool does it automatically with the live spot price.

Why is silver's premium higher than gold's?

Silver is far cheaper per ounce, so the fixed costs of minting, handling and shipping are a much larger share of the price. The same few dollars of fabrication is tiny on a gold coin but large on a silver one.

Is a lower premium always better?

A lower premium means less paid above metal value, but it is only one factor. Dealer reputation, shipping, insurance, buy-back spread, recognisability and taxes matter too. We show the premium so you can compare like with like — we do not give buying advice.

Does the premium include shipping and tax?

No. Enter the metal price of the item only. Shipping, insurance and any sales tax or VAT are separate costs on top of the premium and vary by country and dealer.
