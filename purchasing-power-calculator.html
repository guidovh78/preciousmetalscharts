Measuring your money in gold

Inflation is usually measured against a basket of consumer goods. This tool uses a different, very old yardstick: gold. If a sum of cash bought one ounce decades ago but only buys half an ounce today, then in gold terms that cash has lost half its value — because it now takes twice as many dollars to buy the same metal. It is not the same as official CPI inflation, but it is a concrete way to see how a currency holds up against hard money over time.

Common questions

What does this calculator show?

How many ounces a cash amount could buy in a past year versus today, how much gold-buying power it has lost, and what the same amount would be worth had you held gold.

How is it measured?

We divide your amount by the gold price then and now. If a sum bought one ounce before but half an ounce today, it has lost half its gold-buying power because gold has doubled.

Is gold a perfect inflation hedge?

No. Over decades it has broadly held purchasing power, but it can lag inflation for years and is volatile. This is the historical record, not a guarantee.

Why gold instead of CPI?

Because gold is a market store of value that needs no external index. Measuring cash in gold answers a concrete question — how much metal does my money still buy — a different lens from CPI.
