What the gold-to-silver ratio means

Short, self-contained answers — the basics people search for most.

What is the gold-to-silver ratio?

The gold-to-silver ratio is the number of ounces of silver it takes to buy one ounce of gold, calculated by dividing the gold price by the silver price. It is a quick gauge of how the two metals are priced relative to each other, and it moves continuously as both spot prices change.

What counts as a high or low ratio?

Historically the ratio has ranged from under 35 at silver peaks to above 100 during market stress, with a long-run average roughly in the 55–70 area. Readings well above that band are widely called "high" and are often read as a sign that silver is relatively cheap; low readings suggest the opposite.

How is it calculated?

Divide the current gold price per troy ounce by the current silver price per troy ounce. If gold is 4,000 and silver is 50, the ratio is 80 — it would take 80 ounces of silver to buy one ounce of gold. Because it is a ratio of two prices in the same currency, it is the same number whether you measure in dollars or euros.

Why do people watch it?

Some long-term holders use the ratio as a relative-value signal: swapping between the metals when the ratio reaches an historical extreme. Others simply use it as a single number that summarises the gold–silver relationship at a glance. It is a piece of context, not a recommendation to buy or sell.

Common questions

Has the ratio ever gone above 100?

Yes. The ratio briefly spiked above 100 during the market stress of early 2020, an unusually high reading by historical standards. It has spent most of modern history well below that, which is why a move toward 100 draws attention.

Is a high ratio a signal to buy silver?

A high ratio only tells you silver is cheap relative to gold at that moment — not that either metal will rise or fall. Both can move together. We provide the number and its history as context; we do not give investment advice.

Where does this ratio come from?

The live ratio is calculated from our own ~10-minute-delayed gold and silver spot prices. The historical line is built from our own price archive, with deep history sourced from open data (World Bank), so the long view goes back to the 1970s.

Does the ratio change on weekends?

The ratio updates while global metal markets trade, roughly Sunday evening through Friday afternoon (US Eastern time), and holds steady when those markets are closed — because both underlying spot prices hold steady too.
